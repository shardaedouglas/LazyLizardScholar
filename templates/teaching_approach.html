Structured Problem-Solving

We teach students the "Puzzle Solving Recipe," a framework based on Polya's Four Step Problem Solving Process that gives students a clear, repeatable method for approaching any challenge they encounter.

The Four Steps:
1
Understand the Puzzle

What does the puzzle want you to do? Can you talk about the problem in your own words?

2
Create a Plan

Write an algorithm, draw a picture, work backwards, or solve one small piece at a time.

3
Perform and Perfect

Hunt for one error at a time and retest your plan after every change.

4
Check Your Work

Did your answer solve the puzzle? Now, is there an easier way to do it?

The Art of Debugging

We teach students a structured approach to troubleshooting called the "Debugging Recipe," turning errors into learning opportunities.

Understand the Task at Hand

Do you understand the puzzle prompt? Can you restate it? Do you understand the code you were given?

Pay Attention to Directions & Work

Review directions multiple times. Check your work frequently.

Take it Slow

Add only one element at a time and test the solution before adding more.

One Step at a Time

Watch your program run carefully to see where it goes wrong. What does it do versus what should it do?

Core Computer Science Concepts

Our curriculum introduces fundamental programming concepts through a blend of unplugged activities and online practice.

Algorithms & Sequencing

Definition: An algorithm is a list of steps that you can follow to finish a task.

Unplugged: Graph Paper Programming

Students "program" a partner to reproduce a drawing on a grid using symbolic language, reinforcing the importance of clear, sequential instructions.

Online: Maze Sequence

Students use block-based programming to guide characters through mazes using sequential command blocks.

Loops & Repetition

Definition: A loop is the action of doing something over and over again, making code more concise and efficient.

Unplugged: Getting Loopy

Students participate in a "dance party" where they learn to convert repeating moves into single loop commands.

Online: Bee Loops

Students use loop blocks to program a bee to perform repetitive tasks efficiently.

Functions & Reusability

Definition: A function is a piece of code that you can easily call over and over again, reducing redundancy and organizing code.

Unplugged: Songwriting

Students analyze songs to identify repeating choruses, demonstrating how functions reduce redundancy.

Online: Artist Functions

Students use pre-built functions to create complex drawings with repeating patterns.

Variables & Parameters

Definition: A variable is a placeholder for information that can change, while parameters modify function behavior for specific tasks.

Unplugged: Variables in Envelopes

Students use labeled envelopes to hold values that can change, introducing the core concept of variables.

Online: Artist Variables

Students use variables to make drawings dynamic and parameters to create variety in similar shapes.

Digital Citizenship Integration

Teaching students to be responsible digital citizens is essential to modern computer science education, ensuring they navigate the online world safely and ethically.

Internet Safety Rules

We teach three simple and memorable rules for young students to follow whenever they are online:

1

Always ask your parent (or teacher) first. Seek guidance before visiting new websites or sharing information.

2

Only talk to people you know. Understand the boundary between known friends and strangers online.

3

Stick to places that are just right for you. Recognize age-appropriate websites and avoid content meant for older users.

Understanding Digital Footprints

Students learn that every online action contributes to their Digital Footprint. We teach the crucial difference between safe and unsafe information sharing.

SAFE - Personal Information
  • Your first name
  • Your favorite food
  • Your opinion (shared respectfully)
  • Your hobbies and interests
UNSAFE - Private Information
  • Your full name
  • Your address or phone number
  • Your date of birth
  • Your Social Security number

From Learning to Creating

Our culminating projects mark the transition from structured learning to creative expression, allowing students to synthesize their knowledge and build something personally meaningful.

Creating Stories

Students apply all their coding skills to build animated stories with moving characters, taking ownership of their learning and producing tangible results.

Creating Games

Students design interactive games with user-controlled actions, synthesizing knowledge of sequencing, loops, events, and variables.

The Value of Peer Learning

A key feature of our creative projects is the ability to "Look Under the Hood." When students share their projects, they also share all the code behind it, fostering a powerful collaborative learning environment where students can:

Learn different ways to solve problems by seeing peers' code

Get inspiration for new ideas and features

Debug and build upon each other's projects constructively
